# 📚 Online Bookstore Sales Dashboard
### A Data Analytics Portfolio Project | E-Commerce & Finance

---

## What Is This Project?

This project analyzes sales data from an online bookstore. The goal is simple: **help the business owner understand what's happening in their store — what's selling, who's buying, and whether the business is making money.**

The analysis was done in two steps:

1. **SQL** — used to clean the data and pull the exact numbers needed.
2. **Power BI** — used to turn those numbers into a clear, visual report that anyone can read.

---

## The Dashboard at a Glance

![Book Sales Dashboard](Online_Bookstore_Dashboard.PNG)

The report has one page with everything in one place:

- **4 KPI cards** at the top — the headline numbers
- **A line chart** — sales trends across every month of the year
- **A donut chart** — which book genres are selling the most
- **3 tables** — top customers (this year), top customers (last year), and top-performing books
- **A year slicer** — filter the whole report by 2022, 2023, or 2024

## The 4 Key Numbers (KPIs)

| Metric | Value | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| **Total Sales (GMV)** | $75,628.66 | Total money the store brought in from book sales |
| **Total Orders** | 500 | How many times customers placed an order |
| **Units Sold** | 2,697 | How many individual books were sold |
| **Number of Customers** | 307 | How many unique people bought from the store |

> **GMV (Gross Merchandise Value)** = the total value of all goods sold. This is the top-line revenue number before any costs are subtracted.

## Stakeholder Questions & Answers

---

### Q1: How much money did the store make?

**The store generated $75,628.66 in total sales across 500 orders.**

This is a healthy volume for an online bookstore. With an average order value of roughly **$151 per order**, customers are not just buying one book — they are likely buying multiple titles or higher-priced books per visit. That is a good sign.

---

### Q2: Is the business growing or slowing down?

**The business has seasonal peaks and dips — it is not flat.**

Looking at the monthly sales line chart:

- Sales are **strongest around May**, reaching close to $8,000 in a single month.
- There is a **noticeable dip mid-year** (around June–July), which is common in book retail.
- Sales **recover toward the end of the year**, which aligns with typical holiday buying behavior.

**What this means:** The business is not in decline, but it is not growing steadily either. There are clear peak months and slow months, which gives the owner an opportunity to plan promotions during the slow periods.

---

### Q3: Which types of books are selling the most?

**Romance and Mystery lead the store — together they account for over 34% of all sales.**

Here is the full breakdown by genre:

| Genre | Share of Sales |
|---|---|
| Romance | 17.3% |
| Mystery | 16.91% |
| Fiction | 15.56% |
| Non-Fiction | 15.34% |
| Fantasy | 14.86% |
| Science Fiction | 10.41% |
| Biography | 9.61% |

**What this means:** The store has a well-diversified genre mix — no single genre dominates overwhelmingly, which reduces risk. However, Romance and Mystery are the clear top performers. If the store wants to boost sales quickly, focusing marketing or promotions on these two genres is the safest bet.

---

### Q4: Who are the store's best customers?

**The top spender this year is Kim Turner at $1,398.90, followed by Jonathon Strickland at $1,080.95.**

The top 10 customers collectively represent a significant share of total revenue. These are the store's most valuable relationships.

**Previous year's top spender was Anthony Young at $808.53** — notably lower than this year's top spender, which suggests that either individual spending is increasing year over year, or a new high-value customer segment has emerged.

**What this means:** The store has a loyal core of repeat buyers. These customers should be treated as VIPs — a simple loyalty reward or early-access offer could keep them coming back and spending more.

---

### Q5: What are the best-selling books?

**The top book by revenue is "Integrated secondary access" with $1,104.69 in revenue from 23 units sold.**

Interestingly, the book with the most units sold — "Realigned multi-tasking installation" at 28 units — only generated $594.44, meaning it sells at a lower price point. Meanwhile, "Integrated secondary access" made nearly double that revenue from fewer units because it carries a higher price.

**What this means:** High-unit books and high-revenue books are not always the same. The store can maximize revenue by promoting higher-priced titles, not just the ones that move the most copies.

---

### Q6: Is the business profitable?

**Based on the available data, the business shows strong revenue signals — but profitability cannot be confirmed from sales data alone.**

Here is what the data does tell us:

- $75,628.66 in revenue is a real and meaningful number for an online bookstore.
- 307 customers generating 500 orders means the average customer placed **1.6 orders** — showing signs of repeat purchasing.
- Selling 2,697 units across 500 orders means an average of **5.4 books per order**, which is solid basket size.

**What the data does NOT tell us:** costs. To know if the business is truly profitable, you would need to subtract the cost of goods (what was paid for the books), shipping costs, platform fees, and any operational expenses.

**Honest assessment:** The revenue base is healthy. If the bookstore operates with typical e-commerce margins (30–50%), it is likely profitable. The diversity of genres, repeat customers, and consistent monthly sales all point in the right direction.

---

### Q7: What should the business owner focus on next?

Based on everything in the dashboard, here are three clear priorities:

**1. Fix the mid-year sales dip.**
June and July are clearly slower months. A targeted promotion — a summer reading sale, genre spotlight, or bundle offer — could recover some of that lost revenue.

**2. Reward the top customers.**
The top 10–15 customers are driving a disproportionate share of revenue. A simple loyalty program or personalized email with "books you might like" could increase their spending further.

**3. Double down on Romance and Mystery.**
These two genres lead sales. Expanding the catalog in these categories, or running genre-specific promotions, is the lowest-risk path to growing revenue.

## How to Read This Report (For Non-Technical Readers)

You do not need to know anything about data or technology to use this dashboard. Here is all you need to know:

- The **4 boxes at the top** give you the most important numbers at a glance.
- The **line chart** shows you which months were good and which were slow.
- The **donut chart** shows you which types of books people are buying.
- The **three tables** show you your best customers and best books.
- The **buttons at the top right (2022, 2023, 2024)** let you switch between years to compare.

If a number looks low in one year and high in another, that is your signal to ask: *what did we do differently?*
